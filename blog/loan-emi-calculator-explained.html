Loan EMI Calculator Explained: How Monthly Payments Are Calculated

June 14, 2026 7 min read

Whether it's a car loan, home loan, or personal loan, almost every loan in the world is repaid through EMIs — Equated Monthly Installments. Understanding how this number is calculated helps you make smarter borrowing decisions and avoid surprises.

What is an EMI?

EMI stands for Equated Monthly Installment — a fixed payment amount made by a borrower to a lender at a specified date each month. EMIs are used to pay off both the interest and principal of a loan gradually, so that over a specified number of years, the loan is fully paid off.

The key feature of an EMI is that it stays the same every month for the life of the loan (assuming a fixed interest rate) — even though the proportion going toward interest versus principal changes over time.

The EMI Formula

The standard EMI formula is:

EMI = [P × R × (1+R)^N] ÷ [(1+R)^N − 1]

Where:

  • P = Principal loan amount
  • R = Monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100)
  • N = Number of monthly installments (loan tenure in months)

This formula is derived from the present value of an annuity — essentially calculating the fixed payment that, when discounted back at the monthly interest rate, equals the original loan amount.

Worked Example

Suppose you borrow $10,000 at an annual interest rate of 12%, to be repaid over 24 months.

  • P = 10,000
  • R = 12% ÷ 12 ÷ 100 = 0.01 (1% monthly)
  • N = 24

Plugging into the formula gives an EMI of approximately $470.73 per month. Over 24 months, total payments would be roughly $11,297.52 — meaning total interest paid is approximately $1,297.52.

Why the Interest/Principal Split Changes Over Time

Even though the EMI amount stays constant, the breakdown between interest and principal changes each month. Early in the loan, the outstanding balance is large, so a larger portion of each EMI goes toward interest. As the balance decreases, more of each subsequent EMI goes toward paying down the principal.

This is why making extra payments early in a loan's life has an outsized impact on reducing total interest — you're reducing the principal while interest charges are at their highest proportion.

How Loan Tenure Affects Total Cost

A longer tenure reduces your monthly EMI but increases the total interest paid over the life of the loan, since interest accrues for a longer period. A shorter tenure increases the monthly EMI but reduces total interest paid significantly.

For example, doubling a loan's tenure from 12 to 24 months roughly halves the EMI, but the total interest paid can increase substantially — often by 80-100% or more, depending on the interest rate — because interest compounds over the longer period.

How Interest Rate Affects EMI

Even small differences in interest rate can have a meaningful impact on EMI and total cost, especially for large loans like mortgages with long tenures. A 1% difference in interest rate on a 20-year home loan can translate to tens of thousands of dollars in total interest over the life of the loan — making it worthwhile to shop around for the best rate.

Common Uses for an EMI Calculator

  • Home loans/mortgages — Planning monthly budgets for major property purchases
  • Car loans — Comparing financing offers from different lenders or dealerships
  • Personal loans — Understanding the true cost of borrowing for any purpose
  • Education loans — Planning repayment after graduation
  • Comparing loan offers — Two loans with different rates and tenures can have very different total costs despite similar EMIs
